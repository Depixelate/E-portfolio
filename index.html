Course Learning Outcomes

Throughout ENC 1102, I have developed skills in the following key areas:

1. Generating Inquiry

This outcome is about learning how to ask deep, genuine questions that do not have easy answers. Instead of just picking a topic and reporting facts, it requires us to find a "gap" in the current conversation. We have to look at what others are saying and find a space where we can add something new. It involves exploring lines of inquiry related to writing, rhetoric, or literacy and keeping an open mind about where the research might lead us.

2. Multiple Ways of Writing

Writing is not just black text on a white page. This outcome focuses on the ability to integrate different modes of communication to make an argument stronger. This includes using visuals, layout design, audio, or video. It also means understanding that different situations require different tools. For this class, it meant understanding how to present data visually in a chart or how to design a website like this portfolio to be user-friendly and professional.

3. Information Literacy

We live in a world with too much information, so this outcome is crucial. It involves the ability to find, evaluate, and use sources effectively. It is not just about finding a quote to support a point. It is about checking if a source is credible, relevant, and ethical. It involves understanding the difference between a primary source (like raw data) and a secondary source (like an academic article) and knowing when to use each one to support a research project.

4. Research Genre Production

Every community has its own way of writing, which we call a "genre." This outcome is about producing writing that fits specific public or academic contexts. It demonstrates the ability to navigate the rules, choices, and constraints of these genres. For example, a research proposal has different rules than a casual blog post. Mastering this outcome means being able to analyze a new genre and write within its specific boundaries.

5. Contributing Knowledge

Research is a conversation. This outcome requires us to do more than just summarize what other people have said. We have to draw our own original conclusions based on the primary evidence we collect. It involves analyzing data and explaining what it means, then placing that new insight into conversation with existing research. It is about moving from being a consumer of information to being a creator of new knowledge.

6. Revision

Writing is never perfect in the first draft. This outcome highlights writing as a process rather than a one-time event. It demonstrates the ability to negotiate differences, accept feedback from peers and instructors, and act with intention to rethink and improve writing. It involves looking at the big picture, like the structure and argument, rather than just fixing spelling mistakes.

Final Research Paper

Commit as Genre: The structure and function of commit messages across three GitHub projects

The goal of this final paper was to treat commit messages as a recurring kind of writing. It asks what parts these messages usually have, what parts sometimes appear, what each part does for readers, and how message form differs in different kinds of projects. The goal is to produce clear, practical descriptions that readers with no programming background can understand.

Writing Process for Final Research Paper

The writing process for my final research paper was long and iterative. It started with my First Draft, where I simply tried to get all my ideas down on paper. I combined the research from my annotated bibliography with the primary data I collected from GitHub. At this stage, the paper was messy. The sections did not flow well together, and I was struggling to connect my specific findings about commit messages back to the larger theories of genre analysis.


After completing the first draft, I participated in a Peer Review Workshop. This was a turning point for my paper. My partner pointed out that my "Methods" section was too technical and hard to understand for someone who does not code. They also noted that I had forgotten to include my analysis chart in the body of the text. I used this feedback to rewrite the methods section, removing jargon and explaining terms like "repository" and "merge" more clearly.


For the Final Polish, I focused on the "Revision" outcome. I read the paper out loud to catch awkward sentences and citation errors. I realized that my conclusion was too abrupt, so I added a section discussing the limitations of my study. This process of drafting, getting feedback, and revising helped me produce a much stronger final paper than I could have written on my own.
